Flour Characteristics and the Chopin Alveograph
The Chopin Alveograph is the common international method to assess flour/dough characteristics. It works by blowing a bubble in a carefully created dough sample with specific thickness and dimensions
The alveograph the following outputs
P-index: the tenacity of the flour. How much pressure is required to create a bubble
L-index: the elasticity of the flour. How much can the dough be stretched before the bubble breaks.
G-index: the capacity of the bubble to bulge until it breaks, indicates the rising yield for the flour.
W-index: the energy necessary to inflate the bubble of dough. This is te most common factor used to assess a flour

Resistance extensibility ratio (P/L)
Numerous factors brought about by fermentation and inclusion of other ingredients will affect the properties of gluten, even water quality. Excessively hard water, rich in minerals will make gluten more tenacious reflecting an increase in the P/L ratio. Soft water will encourage the opposite effect and make dough softer and more extensible which would display a lower P/L ratio.

W-Index
Flours with a higher W-index are so called stronger because characterized by higher content of the proteins (gliadin and glutenin) that form gluten when in contact with water. That means that with high W-index flours you can increase the hydration of your baking products, because your flour can take all that water and in fact make gluten.

Flours can be grouped based on their W-index as follows:
Less than 90 not suited for baking
90-160 with low rising capacity ideal for cookies, crackers, breadsticks, etc.
160-250 suited for short fermentations like focaccia or pizza
250-310 a safe range to make several preparations as bread, croissants, more pizza and focaccia
310-370 for long fermentation and rich baking products as panettone, pandoro, colombe.
Additional Info
Wheat Structure
Wheat grains contain germ, bran, and endosperm

Endosperm is the white stuff, and the germ and bran make it browner. So the lower the bran and germ content, the whiter the flour is, and vice versa. Composition is typically:

80-85% endosperm
10-14% bran
2-3% germ

Manitoba Flour
Manitoba flour has a high glutenin and gliadin content and creates a highly elastic and consistent dough. This means it is better suited to bread-making processes or for making products that require a long leavening time.
It can be mixed with weaker flours to increase the strength and is commonly used to make panettone.

Gluten Composition vs Flour Strength
Gluten consists of proteins gliadin and glutenin, the latter providing the main structure which supports the dough. A high gluten flour will not necessarily be "strong" if it contains an unusually high ratio of gliadin to glutenin

Other factors affecting dough qualities
Numerous factors brought about by fermentation and inclusion of other ingredients will affect the properties of gluten, even water quality. Excessively hard water, rich in minerals will make gluten more tenacious reflecting an increase in the P/L ratio. Soft water will encourage the opposite effect and make dough softer and more extensible which would display a lower P/L ratio.

Ash Content
Ash content is an indicator of how refined (white) the flour is. The word ash here is used to mean mineral content.

Wheat is around 2% ash, 0.35% in the endosperm and the other 1.65% in the germ and bran. Ash is not in itself important in baking, but as endosperm is relatively lower in ash, a lower the ash content indicates a more refined flour.
