Bread
GENERAL TIPS
Add liquid first when using a machine to mix
Crumble the fresh yeast by rubbing it with some dry flour between your hands
Cold proofing or warm on crumb openness: Neither provides a clearly more open crumb
Autolyse or not: Bit more oven spring with autolyse, but not very clear. Autolsye more sour
Preheat the oven or not: Yes, preheat always
Oven hot initally the colder, or steady temperature:Very hot initially gives thicker base crust. otherwise not much difference
Scoring:
  • Score with a corner of the blade
  • Bread Code channel suggests putting in oven for 30 seconds, then scoring is easier (but...real bakers don't need this trick)
  • Scoring in one flamboyant swish is the goal, as a half-assed hacky score doesn't open well
  • Scoring angle is important: 45 degrees to the surface of the dough (not to horizontal) is often quoted
  • Baguette scoring guide - here and here. Scores ovelapped one third, think of cutting into the dough rather than following a line, horizontal blade
Tips for improving oven spring:
  • Dough Strength: improve this
  • Fermentation needs to be on point: Too much is as bad as too little (measuring cylinder to kepp an accurate eye). pH meter would be grwat for this as it gives a very accurate indication of fermentation status, but is expensive
  • Baking setup: Steam to delay crust formation. Dutch oven, or steam generating tray below with inverted baking sheet above to trap steam. Fan off so it doesn't suck steam straight out, and maybe hinder venting also
Autolyse
Supposed advantages of Autolyse
  • Less kneading
  • Faster fermentation
  • Better browning, as more sugar available
  • More open crumb
  • Feels lighter when eating
Important Points
  • Autolysing too long eventually breaks down your gluten
  • The more gluten you have, the longer you can autolyse
  • The hotter it is, the faster the autolyse gets out of control and breaks down your dough
  • Rough limit suggestions (all flours are different) - 10 hours at 20C. And don't autolyse if you do low yeast very long fermentation e.g. neapolitan pizza
Sourdough
General Info
  • Sourness comes from lactic acid produced by bacteria, fermentation power comes from multiplying yeasts. There's a bit of a yeast vs. bacteria battle going on
  • Controlling sourness: Firm starters will have less acidity than liquid ones, so you get more yeasts for less sourness i.e. Your bread will be less sour in the end
  • Feeding your starter: After feeding the starter, the yeast goes wild for a bit, after that the bacteria catch up and eventually overtake. If you wait a long time after feeding, your yeast power reduces and acidity increases. Use the starter within 8 hours (less also fine) of feeding if you want good rising power
  • Water stored starters: Italians use firm starters as they don't like acidity in the bread, and they store these firm starters in water which dilutes the acidity further
  • Yeast is active down to -2C, the bacteria is very inactive below 20C
